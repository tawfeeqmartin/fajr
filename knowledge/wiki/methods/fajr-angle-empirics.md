# Fajr Angle — Empirical Observations

The 18°/19° Fajr angle cluster (used by ISNA's predecessor methods, MWL, Egyptian, Morocco, JAKIM, Karachi, MoonsightingCommittee post-2018) has periodically been challenged by communities reporting "Fajr is too early." This entry summarises the empirical literature on what Fajr looks like at a real horizon, why those community complaints are real, and why they nonetheless do not invalidate the calculated 18° angle.

The key resolution is that the calculated angle is correct; the discrepancy that observers report is **light pollution**, which delays the visible onset of dawn by 20–30 minutes inside lit cities relative to the actual sky-level dawn.

---

## Empirical study — Aabed 2015 (Jordan, naked-eye, 12 sessions)

**Citation:** [Aabed, 2015] — Prof. Abdulkader M. Abed, *Determining the beginning of the true dawn (Al-Fajr Al-Sadek) observationally by the naked eye in Jordan*, Jordan Journal for Islamic Studies, v. 11(2). Archived at [`knowledge/raw/papers/2026-05-01-astronomycenter/aabed_2015_fajr_empirical.pdf`](../../raw/papers/2026-05-01-astronomycenter/aabed_2015_fajr_empirical.pdf).

A team from the **Jordanian Astronomical Society**, with their friends and several *ulamāʾ*, conducted twelve naked-eye true-dawn observation sessions across **four localities in Jordan** during the year **1430/1431 AH (2009/2010 AD)**. Conditions varied: clear, dusty, and cloudy nights were all included; ten of the twelve sessions had darkness adequate for unaided-eye observation.

**Method controls:**
- Observation away from illuminated cities (light pollution explicitly identified as a confounder)
- Multiple observers per session for inter-observer agreement
- *Tayakkun* (group concurrence on visibility) recorded as the strict criterion

**Headline result (verbatim from the English abstract):**

> *True fajr was observed 4–5 minutes after the beginning of A'than ul Fajr in Amman by part of the observers, and one time, 5 minutes by the whole group (tayakkun). If the observing conditions were a little better, we could have seen it at the beginning of A'than. Thus, it is recommended to keep the time of A'than ul Fajr as it is now; i.e. **when the sun is at 18° below the eastern horizon**. It is also accepted to delay A'than by 5 minutes only to be sure of the right timing (tayakkun).*

**Critical operational finding (verbatim):**

> *It was clear that true dawn must be observed in complete darkness away from illuminated cities. Consequently, **timing of the beginning of the true dawn in cities should be rejected because it is usually 20–30 minutes later than the observed time**.*

This is the resolution to the recurring "Fajr is too early" claim that surfaces in many Muslim communities. The complaint is genuine — observers in lit cities really do not see dawn at the calculated time — but the cause is light pollution suppressing the visibility of the early dawn glow, not an error in the calculated 18° angle. The 18° calculation correctly identifies when the sky reaches astronomical-twilight darkness on a dark horizon; whether a particular observer can *perceive* that depends on local sky brightness.

## Empirical study — Almisnid 2012 (Qassim, instrumented)

**Citation:** [Almisnid + Al-Sukakir, 2012] — *Determining the Beginning of Fajer Prayer Time in Qassim Area Practically*. Archived at [`knowledge/raw/papers/2026-05-01-astronomycenter/almisnid_2012.pdf`](../../raw/papers/2026-05-01-astronomycenter/almisnid_2012.pdf).

Independent observational study in Saudi Arabia's Qassim region using:
- **Sky Quality Meter (SQM)** measurements of sky brightness
- Long-exposure photography (Canon EOS 5D Mark II, 30s exposure, f/2.8, ISO 32000)
- Naked-eye observations
- Ground-truth correlation with calculated 18° times

The Sky Quality Meter measures sky brightness in units of magnitudes per arcsecond². The team recorded SQM readings of 21.23–21.56 mag/arcsec² as the threshold for observable true dawn — a quantitative anchor that previously had only qualitative ("complete darkness") description.

The instrumented observations are consistent with Aabed 2015's qualitative findings: the 18° calculation correctly identifies the astronomical dawn boundary; observable dawn from inside lit areas trails the calculated time.

**Bonus:** the paper documents the false-dawn (zodiacal light / *al-fajr al-kāzib* / *al-fajr al-burūjī*) phenomenon that Hadith literature carefully distinguishes from true dawn (*al-fajr al-sādiq*) — relevant to discussions of why two-stage dawn appears even at the 12° depression that some methods use.

## Scholarly review — Odeh 2010 *Astronomical and Juristic Problems*

**Citation:** [Odeh, 2010] — Mohammad Shawkat Odeh, *Astronomical and Juristic Problems Regarding Prayer Times*. Archived at [`knowledge/raw/papers/2026-05-01-astronomycenter/odeh_2010_salat_problems.pdf`](../../raw/papers/2026-05-01-astronomycenter/odeh_2010_salat_problems.pdf).

Comprehensive review covering true vs false Fajr (astronomical and juristic perspectives), Ghalas (Dark Fajr) vs Isfar (Bright Fajr), late prominent astronomers' positions on the correct Fajr angle, and synthesis of recent observational studies. Odeh quotes the Moonsighting Committee's 2009-09-06 announcement directly: *"We also recommend 18 degrees for 'Isha prayer"* — a significant institutional convergence on 18° for both Fajr and Isha after several decades of methodological drift in North American practice.

The paper is essentially a compiled wiki on the prayer-time angle topic and is the recommended single citation for fajr's `methods.js` angle table choices.

## The 2018–2019 Moonsighting Committee controversy — Sumeat 2019

**Citation:** [Sumeat, 2019] — Dr. Mustafa bin Hamid bin Sumeat, *The Claim of Error in the Time of Fajr Prayer through the Texts of Jurists and Astronomers*. Archived at [`knowledge/raw/papers/2026-05-01-astronomycenter/sumeat_2019_fajr_error.pdf`](../../raw/papers/2026-05-01-astronomycenter/sumeat_2019_fajr_error.pdf).

In October 2018, Moonsighting.com — a widely-followed reference among English-speaking Muslims — announced (paraphrasing the quoted English statement): *"The correct Fajr time begins when sun is 18 degrees below the horizon. Inshallah, we shall correct the information given in the name of ISNA"*. This was a public reversal of earlier recommendations that had drifted toward 12°–15°. Sumeat 2019 engages with the controversy from inside the discipline, surveying both jurist (*fuqahā*) and astronomer (*falakiyyīn*) positions on the alleged Fajr-time error.

The takeaway for fajr: the 18° angle is not just astronomically defensible (per al-Biruni's classical determination), it is *currently* the convergence point of the major contemporary Islamic-astronomical bodies — ICOP, MWL, ISNA-via-Moonsighting Committee, JAKIM, Egyptian General Survey, Morocco's Habous (at 19°). The "Fajr is wrong" community sentiment is sociological more than astronomical; the engineering response is to correctly calculate 18° (or the regional cousin) and surface a documentation note about light pollution, not to lower the angle.

## fajr's position

| Question | fajr's choice | Supporting citation |
|---|---|---|
| What Fajr angle is correct? | 18°/19° cluster (regional) | Odeh 2010 review, Aabed 2015 empirical, Sumeat 2019 review of consensus |
| Are urban observation reports of "late dawn" valid? | Yes — but the cause is light pollution, not calculation error | Aabed 2015 explicit |
| Should fajr lower the angle to match urban observations? | **No** | Would invert ihtiyat (Fajr earlier than fasting-safe boundary in dark sky); contradicts the modern institutional consensus |
| Should fajr surface a caveat to the user? | **Yes** | Add an output note explaining the light-pollution gap when the user's location is plausibly urban |

The output-note implementation is tracked in `src/engine.js` and lands as part of v1.2.x.
