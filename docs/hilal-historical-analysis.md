## Headline finding — bimodal alignment

The aggregate ~46% rate is *not* a coin flip — it's a sharp bimodal pattern that **empirically demonstrates the wasāʾil/ʿibādāt distinction the project is built on**:

| Committee cluster | N | Odeh | Yallop | Shaukat |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Strict naked-eye sighting** (Pakistan, Morocco, India, Iran, Indonesia) | 33 | **81.8%** | **87.9%** | **84.8%** |
| **Accepts witness testimony / calculation** (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Egypt, Qatar, Turkey) | 45 | 15.6% | 15.6% | 20.0% |

**Read this carefully.** fajr's three astronomical criteria are calibrated to predict naked-eye visibility under reasonable sky conditions — the same standard the strict-sighting committees apply. So when the moon is genuinely too young or too low to be seen, fajr says "not visible" and the strict committees say "not sighted" — they agree at 80–100%. When the moon is borderline-visible, the witness-testimony committees often declare "sighted" anyway (witness testimony is itself a valid scholarly basis under their methodology); fajr's astronomical criteria can't predict those announcements because they're not predictions of astronomy in the first place.

This is exactly the wasāʾil (means) / ʿibādāt (worship) distinction made empirical: software (astronomy) reliably predicts what astronomy predicts. It cannot — and should not try to — predict what scholars decide based on testimony and authority.

**Practical implication for downstream apps:** for users in strict-sighting countries (Pakistan, Morocco, India, Iran), fajr's criteria are a reasonable predictor of next-day announcements (~80–100%). For users in witness-testimony countries (Saudi, UAE, Egypt), fajr's criteria predict "would the moon be naked-eye visible," not "what will the committee announce" — those are different questions, and the right primary signal is the user's local committee, not fajr.

## Interpretation

**What this analysis is.** A measurement of how often each of fajr's three astronomical visibility criteria coincides with each committee's announced decision. The dataset is curated from primary news reporting and represents documented (not inferred) decisions.

**What this analysis is NOT.** A claim that any criterion is "correct." Committee decisions are *fiqh* judgments combining astronomical possibility with witness testimony and methodological tradition. A criterion that disagrees with a particular committee may still be astronomically defensible — and a committee that announces "sighted" for a sub-Danjon-impossible crescent is not making an astronomical claim, it is exercising scholarly authority. fajr is wasail (means); committee decisions are ibadat-adjacent (acts that govern worship). They occupy different domains and are not commensurate.

**What this is useful for.** 

1. Showing downstream apps which criterion best aligns with a given regional authority, so they can pick the criterion their users care about.
2. Identifying *patterns* of disagreement — committees that systematically accept sub-Danjon crescents (UAE in 1445, Egypt in some borderline cases) vs. those that systematically require strict naked-eye conditions (Pakistan, Morocco, Iran).
3. Tracking, over time, whether fajr's criteria become more or less aligned with committee decisions as both methodology and committee practice evolve.

**Dataset growth path.** PRs welcome to expand `eval/data/hilal-observations.json` with additional Hijri month onsets, additional countries per onset, or higher confidence levels for existing entries (with primary source citations). Targets: backfill Hijri 1430–1440 (~10 years × 3 events × ~10 countries ≈ 300 additional decisions); ongoing forward-fill as each new Hijri month is announced.
